About & AI Metrics Guide

The History of MENACE

MENACE (Machine Educable Noughts and Crosses Engine) was built in 1960 by Donald Michie. Long before electronic computing was accessible, he proved a physical machine could "learn" through trial and error using 304 matchboxes and glass beads.

How the AI Metrics Work

This implementation tracks live mathematical performance across three custom metrics:

  • AI Efficiency (%): A rolling track of the last 20 games. Because Tic-Tac-Toe is a solved game, both a Win and a Draw are mathematically optimal outcomes for MENACE. If it wins or draws, it scores 1; if it loses, it scores 0. Efficiency represents the percentage of optimal games out of the last 20.
  • Bead Delta (Graph Y-Axis): Measures the total net change of beads in the 1st matchbox (the opening move) relative to its baseline of 72 beads. A rising line means it is accumulating rewards and systematically abandoning losing pathways.
  • Hardcoded Incentives: To prevent breaking the brain constraints, the learning vectors are structurally frozen: Win (+3 beads), Draw (+1 bead), and Loss (-1 bead).

Engine Controls Guide

1. Play Mode

Select Play to challenge MENACE manually. Tap any empty grid squircle to make a move as X.

2. Train Mode

Select Train to let the engine auto-simulate games against an algorithmic script, accelerating its reinforcement data paths.

3. Pace Controls

Use the Speed Slider when training to control simulation speed. Slide right to maximize compute velocity or left to watch games unfold sequentially.
